\begin{abstract}
Primitive recursion is usually introduced as a class of total functions
on natural numbers, but the same idea extends to richer inductive data
and higher-order programs.  We present an Agda formalization of
primitive recursion in categorical terms, using point-free first-order
and higher-order calculi, together with equational theories, setoid
models, interpretation soundness, verified translations between
paramorphism-primitive and fold-primitive presentations, and
representative instances for natural numbers, words, trees, and
many-sorted signatures.

The formalization is organized around small core calculi.  Each hom-set
is equipped with a setoid equality containing the categorical laws,
product and coproduct laws, functoriality and strength laws, and
recursion computation and uniqueness principles.  Higher-order calculi
add exponentials and their beta/eta laws.  We use setoids rather than
quotients and keep categorical distributivity as
primitive in the first-order presentation and derived from exponentials
in the higher-order presentation.

All definitions and proofs are checked by Agda using the safe fragment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Primitive recursive functions are usually introduced as total computable
functions on natural numbers.  They have been part of recursion theory
since the early work of Skolem and Curry: Curry coined the name
``primitive recursive'', but attributes the underlying idea to Skolem's
recursive arithmetic~\cite{skolem23,curry41:_formal_recur_arith}.
Kleene's work on general recursive functions made the contrast between
primitive recursion and unrestricted recursion a standard one in
computability theory~\cite{kleene36:_gener_recur_funct_natur_number}.
Even in this classical setting, the boundary is mathematically
interesting: well-known total functions such as Ackermann's function,
Knuth's up-arrow notation, and Conway's arrow notation are not primitive
recursive as first-order functions on natural numbers, but they become
definable in higher-order systems such as G\"odel's
System~T~\cite{szasz1991machine,knuth76:_mathem_comput_scien,conway96:_book_number,DBLP:journals/corr/Widemann16,goedel58}.

The usual textbook presentation hides a useful structural fact.  A
primitive recursive program is already close to a categorical term: it is
built point-free from base functions, projections, composition, and a primitive
recursion operator.  Once stated this way, there is little reason to tie
the construction to natural numbers alone.  The same pattern applies to
words, ranked trees, and many-sorted algebraic terms; multiple arguments
and structured results are accounted for by products; and higher-order
primitive recursion adds function spaces, as in System~T.  Much of the
literature on recursion schemes studies the generalized setting inside a
typed lambda calculus~\cite{DBLP:books/cu/Ha2016,DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerFP91,DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerH95}.
Our goal is to keep the point-free categorical style visible and to make
the corresponding metatheory executable and checkable.

This paper presents an Agda formalization of that metatheory.  The
formalization is organized around small target calculi.  The
first-order calculus \PRFO{} describes
primitive recursion over polynomial inductive types in a distributive
category.  The higher-order calculus \PRHO{} extends the same type and
arrow structure with exponentials, giving a point-free target for
System~T-style programs.  Each target is treated as a syntactic category:
objects are closed type codes, arrows are intrinsically typed terms, and
the categorical laws are represented by an explicit generated equality
rather than quotienting the syntax.

Mechanizing this account forces several choices that are easy to gloss
over on paper.  Models must carry hom-setoid equality and prove the laws
of the target calculus.  Primitive recursion can be presented either by
paramorphisms, which expose the original subdata to the step, or by
folds, which expose only recursive results.  First-order targets need
distributivity as an axiom, while in the higher-order target the
canonical distributivity map is derivable from exponentials.  Concrete
source languages compile into these targets and inherit semantic
correctness from the model interface.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The paper has three high-level conceptual contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
\item We define point-free categorical target calculi for primitive
  recursion: first-order and higher-order variants, each in
  paramorphism-primitive and fold-primitive form, sharing one typed
  language of products, coproducts, polynomial inductive types, and
  closed-domain exponentials in the higher-order case.
\item We give equational theories and law-bearing setoid models for the
  calculi, together with interpretation soundness.  There is a
  model-level \PRHO{} distributivity theorem: every model satisfying the
  \PRHO{} interface validates the canonical distributivity isomorphism
  derived from exponentials.
\item We verify that the targets host the motivating source languages:
  primitive recursion on natural numbers, words, ranked trees,
  many-sorted signatures, and a System~T fragment all compile to the
  appropriate core calculus with semantic correctness and, where
  applicable, source-equation preservation.
\end{enumerate}
Several subsidiary technical results support these contributions: formal
translations between paramorphism-primitive and fold-primitive
presentations preserve equations and round-trip up to the generated
equational theory; contextual simply typed syntax is related to the
point-free target by compile/reify translations; the first-order target
embeds into the higher-order target; the natural-number compiler extends
to a functor from \(\mathbf{PREC}\) into the finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat}
subcategory of the \PRFO{} target; and concrete representation theorems
connect source evaluators to the executable set interpretation.

\section{Motivation and Running Examples}
\label{sec:motivation}

Before turning to the Agda artifact, we spell out the mathematical
pattern that the artifact abstracts.  The starting point is the familiar
class of primitive recursive functions on natural numbers.  In the usual
presentation~\cite{martin2019logik,wiki:Primitive_recursive_function},
an \(n\)-ary program denotes a function
\(\mathbb{N}^n \to \mathbb{N}\).  The basic programs are zero,
successor, and projections; larger programs are built by composition and
by a primitive-recursion constructor.

The source language \AgdaFile{PR-Nat} formalizes this grammar as an
Agda datatype \(\AgdaDatatype{PR}\ n\), where \(n\) is the arity of the
program.  Its constructor \(P(g,h)\) builds a program of arity \(1+n\).
The first input is the induction argument; \(g\) handles the zero case
using the remaining \(n\) parameters, and \(h\) handles the successor
case using the value obtained for the predecessor, the predecessor
itself, and the same parameters.  Addition is the smallest example: it
is specified by induction on the first argument,
\[
  \begin{array}{rcl}
  \mathsf{add}(0,y) &=& y,\\
  \mathsf{add}(x+1,y) &=& \mathsf{suc}(\mathsf{add}(x,y)).
  \end{array}
\]
In the syntax of \AgdaFile{PR-Nat}, the corresponding program is
generated from one projection for the base case and successor applied to
the predecessor value in the step case:
\[
  \mathsf{add}
  =
  P(\pi_0,\; \mathsf{suc} \circ \pi_0)
  \quad:\quad \mathbb{N}^2 \to \mathbb{N}.
\]
Here the first \(\pi_0\) selects the remaining argument \(y\) in the
zero case, while the second \(\pi_0\) selects the value already obtained
for \(x\) in the successor case.  The notation is already point-free:
variables are represented only through projections, tupling, and
composition.  This is the first categorical reading of ordinary
primitive recursion.  Types are objects, programs are arrows, products
account for multiple arguments, and the primitive-recursion constructor
is an arrow former with a computation law.

Once stated this way, very little is special about natural numbers.
The natural numbers are the initial algebra of the polynomial functor
\[
  F(X) = 1 + X,
\]
where the left summand represents zero and the right summand represents
successor.  The same pattern applies to any strictly positive polynomial
functor.  A list of letters from a finite alphabet \(A\), for instance,
is the initial algebra of
\[
  F(X) = 1 + A \times X.
\]
The word operation \(\mathsf{append}\) follows the same induction
pattern on the first word: the empty-word case returns the second
argument, and the letter case prefixes the current letter onto the value
obtained for the tail,
\[
  \begin{array}{rcl}
  \mathsf{append}(\epsilon,w) &=& w,\\
  \mathsf{append}(a :: u,w) &=& a :: \mathsf{append}(u,w).
  \end{array}
\]
The point-free program has the same shape as addition, except that
successor has been replaced by the family of letter constructors.  The
word source language therefore has one step handler for each leading
letter.

Ranked trees give the next example.  A signature with constructor set
\(\Sigma\) and arity function
\(\mathsf{ar} : \Sigma \to \mathbb{N}\) determines the polynomial
\[
  F(X) = \sum_{c \in \Sigma} X^{\mathsf{ar}(c)}.
\]
A primitive recursive tree program supplies one handler per constructor.
For a binary node \(c\), the handler receives one induction hypothesis
for each child, and in the paramorphism presentation it also receives
the original children.  This extra access is useful for programs that
need to rebuild or inspect the input tree while accumulating information.
The many-sorted version replaces \(X\) by a family of carrier sets
indexed by sorts and replaces a single arity with a vector of input sorts
for each constructor.

The first-order core, \PRFO{}, abstracts over these examples.  Its types
include finite products and coproducts together with inductive types
\(\mu F\) for polynomial functors \(F\).  Its arrows are categorical
combinators plus functorial action, strength, and either a primitive
paramorphism \(P\) or a primitive fold \(F\).  An instance module is a
checked translation from one concrete source language, such as
\AgdaFile{PR-Nat} or the word and tree languages, into this core.  The
translation shows that the concrete language is not a separate calculus:
it is an instance of the same point-free structure.

The next step is higher order.  G\"odel's System~T~\cite{goedel58}, in
Harper's textbook presentation~\cite[Chapter~9]{DBLP:books/cu/Ha2016},
extends simply typed lambda calculus with natural numbers and their
recursor.  Our higher-order core, \PRHO{}, adds
exponentials to the first-order categorical language, yielding a
point-free counterpart of function types, abstraction, and application.
This makes it possible to connect System~T directly to \PRHO{} and then
to view simply typed lambda calculus with polynomial inductive types as
the contextual presentation of the same structure.
Because the higher-order type language extends the first-order one, the
artifact also includes a structural embedding of \PRFO{} into \PRHO{}:
first-order type codes are lifted to higher-order codes, arrows are
translated homomorphically, and generated equations are preserved.

\section{Core Calculi}
\label{sec:core-calculi}

The artifact is organized around four closely related point-free core
calculi.  The first-order calculi, \PRFO{} and \PRFOF{}, have finite
products, finite coproducts, polynomial inductive types, functorial
action, strength, and an eliminator for inductive types.  The
higher-order calculi, \PRHO{} and \PRHOF{}, extend the same syntax with
exponentials.  The suffix-free calculi take paramorphism as primitive;
the \(F\)-suffixed calculi take fold as primitive.

\subsection{Target Categories}
\label{sec:target-categories}

The target of compilation is not Agda's category of types.  Each core
calculus presents a syntactic category.  Write
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{FO}}^P\),
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{FO}}^F\),
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{HO}}^P\), and
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{HO}}^F\) for the categories presented by the four
calculi.  In each case, objects are closed type codes
\(A : \AgdaDatatype{Ty}\ \AgdaBound{md}\ 0\).  Morphisms
\(A \to B\) are arrows of the corresponding intrinsic syntax modulo the
generated equality relation described in
Section~\ref{sec:equations-models}; in Agda, this quotient is represented
as a hom-setoid rather than as a quotient type.

Thus \(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{FO}}^P\) and
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{FO}}^F\) are the first-order target categories:
they have finite products and coproducts, the type-code endofunctors with
their functorial action and strength, chosen initial-algebra objects
\(\mu G\), and either paramorphisms or folds satisfying their laws.
Likewise, \(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{HO}}^P\) and
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathsf{HO}}^F\) add closed-domain exponentials and their
beta/eta laws.  The instance compilers land in these syntactic
categories; the set interpreter and the model interfaces later in the
paper are semantics of these categories, not the targets themselves.

\subsection{Shared Type Language}
\label{sec:shared-type-language}

All four calculi share the same type language, parameterized by a mode.
The mode index \AgdaBound{md} prevents exponentials from appearing in first-order
types, while allowing the rest of the grammar to be reused unchanged.
The \(0\) in the first argument of the exponential constructor is
deliberate: the domain of a function type must be closed.  The index
\(n\) tracks the variables over which a type code acts functorially.
Those variables may occur in the codomain of an exponential, but
occurrences in the domain would be contravariant and would invalidate
the generic \AgdaInductiveConstructor{fmap} and
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength} structure.  Thus a higher-order
type operator may contain function spaces \(A \Rightarrow G\) only when
\(A\) has no free type variables.
The constructor \AgdaInductiveConstructor{ind} binds one type variable
in the code for a polynomial functor and forms its least fixed point.

\subsection{First-Order Core}
\label{sec:core-calculi-fo}

The arrow syntax is intrinsic: the source and target types are indices
of the Agda datatype.  The first-order core with primitive paramorphisms
is shown below.  The constructors up to \AgdaInductiveConstructor{`case}
are the usual categorical structure for finite products and coproducts.
The constructor \AgdaInductiveConstructor{dist-+-×} is included as
first-order infrastructure, following the standard notion of a
distributive category~\cite{cockett_1993}.  The constructors
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{fmap} and \AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength}
make polynomial functors available at the term level, and
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{P} is the primitive paramorphism operator.  We
later require \AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength} to be natural in both
arguments; the derived operation \AgdaFunction{pmap} below is precisely
the composite of this right strength with functorial action.  We write
\AgdaBound{G}\AgdaSpace{}[ \AgdaBound{T}\AgdaSpace{}] for
substituting \AgdaBound{T} for the bound type variable in
\AgdaBound{G}.

{\small
\CorePRFOSyntax*
}

The type of \AgdaInductiveConstructor{P} is the categorical version of
primitive induction.  Given an input of type \AgdaDatatype{ind}
\AgdaBound{G} and parameters \AgdaBound{U}, the step arrow receives the
one-layer unconstructing of the inductive value where recursive positions
carry both the already computed result of type \AgdaBound{T} and the
original substructure of type \AgdaDatatype{ind} \AgdaBound{G}.  This is
the paramorphism distinction: a step can inspect the original recursive
subdata as well as the induction hypotheses.

The fold-primitive first-order calculus has the same categorical
constructors, but replaces \AgdaInductiveConstructor{P} by
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{F}.  Its step receives only the functorial
layer of already computed results, not the original substructures.

\subsection{Higher-Order Core}
\label{sec:core-calculi-ho}

The higher-order calculi reuse the same shared type grammar and
point-free arrow structure, but add exponentials.  In the
paramorphism-primitive presentation, the two additional arrow
constructors are the point-free counterparts of abstraction and
application.

{\small
\CorePRHOExponentials
}

One reason to separate the first- and higher-order cores is that some
categorical structure can be primitive in one presentation and derived
in another.  In \PRFO{}, distributivity is a constructor.  In \PRHO{},
it is definable from exponentials, case analysis, lambda abstraction,
and application, following the standard cartesian-closed construction
for coproduct/product distributivity~\cite{https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.1406.0961}.
The helper \AgdaFunction{theta} is the point-free uncurrying map: given
\(f : U \to (V \Rightarrow T)\), it maps \(U \times V\) to
\((V \Rightarrow T) \times V\) and then applies the resulting function.

{\small
\CorePRHODerivedDist
}

The model-level status of this derived arrow is stated after the model
interface has been introduced, in
Section~\ref{sec:prho-model-distributivity}.

and fold-primitive presentations define the same theory up to the
generated equality.

\section{Equational Theories and Setoid Models}
\label{sec:equations-models}

The datatype of arrows in Section~\ref{sec:core-calculi} is deliberately
intensional: two arrows are the same Agda value only when they have the
same syntax.  This is too strict for the mathematical theories.  For
example, composition with the identity should not change an arrow, and
the eliminator for products should be determined by its two projections.
Instead of quotienting the syntax, the formalization keeps raw arrows and
defines, for each calculus, an indexed equality relation generated by the
expected equations.  The first-order paramorphism calculus is
representative:

{\small
\CorePRFOEquivalence*
}

This relation is an object-language equality between parallel arrows.
Its first constructors make it an equivalence relation, and the next
constructors say that equality is preserved by the term formers.  The
remaining constructors, not all shown here, are the actual laws of the
calculus.  They include the category laws for identity and composition;
the beta and eta laws for products and coproducts; the beta and eta laws
for exponentials in the higher-order calculi; the functoriality and
strength laws for type operators; the distributivity laws in the
first-order calculus where distributivity is primitive; and the
computation and uniqueness principles for primitive recursion.

For structurally generated type operators, the higher-order theory also
contains two computation laws.  The law \AgdaFunction{fmap-βᶜ} identifies
primitive \AgdaInductiveConstructor{fmap} with the derived structural
operation \AgdaFunction{fmapᶜ}; \AgdaFunction{strength-βᶜ} does the same
for \AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength} and
\AgdaFunction{strengthᶜ}.  These laws keep the primitive operations
abstract in the general core, but allow concrete polynomial functors such
as \(1+X\) to compute inside the ordinary \PRHO{} equality.  This is what
lets instance-level equations, including the System~T natural-number
recursor equations, be proved without adding a private target equality for
that source language.

For paramorphisms, the two characteristic laws are:

{\small
\CorePRFOParaLaws*
}

The beta law says how a paramorphism behaves after one constructor
step: the recursive call is exposed through \AgdaFunction{paraArgs},
which supplies both the recursive results and the original subdata to
the step function.  The uniqueness law gives the corresponding induction
principle for arrows out of an inductive type.  Thus recursion is not
specified by an evaluator external to the syntax; it is specified by the
equations that characterize the primitive arrow.

Two derived PR-HO lemmas package common uses of these laws.  The theorem
\(\AgdaFunction{P-βᶜ}\) specializes the paramorphism beta law to a
structural functor by first expanding \AgdaInductiveConstructor{fmap} and
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength}; \(\AgdaFunction{P-parameterʳ}\)
states that a map on the parameter object can be pushed through a
paramorphism.  The System~T equation proof uses these lemmas to derive the
zero and successor recursor equations from the generic \PRHO{} laws.

The semantic side follows the same separation between raw structure and
equality.  A structure provides an interpretation of the object-language
types as objects and of each primitive arrow former as an operation on
semantic arrows.  A model adds a relation
\(\AgdaOperator{\AgdaField{\_≈ᴹ\_}}\) on every hom-type, proves that this
relation is an equivalence, and validates each generated law.  The
interpretation function is defined by recursion on raw syntax, while the
soundness theorem maps a derivation \(f \approx g\) in the generated
equality to a proof that their interpretations are related by
\(\AgdaOperator{\AgdaField{\_≈ᴹ\_}}\).  This makes equality a parameter
of the model rather than forcing every model to use Agda's judgmental or
propositional equality.

relations as standard-library setoids.  For the first-order
paramorphism models, the construction is:

This packaging is mostly administrative, but it is important for the
shape of the formalization.  It lets later developments reason about
hom-objects extensionally, while the syntax remains available for

proofs of equality preservation.  The same pattern is repeated for the
fold-primitive variants and for the higher-order calculi; only the list
of primitive operations and laws changes.

\section{Paramorphisms and Folds}
\label{sec:paramorphisms-folds}

The preceding sections treat \(P\) and \(F\) as two alternative primitive
choices.  This section explains why the artifact contains both.  A fold,
or catamorphism, is the simpler eliminator: its step is given a layer
\(G[T]\) of recursive results and parameters \(U\), and returns a result
of type \(T\)~\cite{DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerFP91}.  A paramorphism is more
informative: its step is given a layer \(G[T \times \mu G]\), so every
recursive position contains both the recursive result and the original
substructure.  The paramorphism presentation is convenient for compiling
primitive-recursion source languages, where successor and tree
constructors often expose the predecessor or child in addition to the
recursive value.  The fold presentation is convenient as a conventional
categorical interface, including in higher-order settings with
exponentials~\cite{DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerH95}.

The two presentations are nevertheless interdefinable.  If \(P\) is
primitive, a fold is obtained by discarding the remembered original
subterm before invoking \(P\).  The excerpt begins by opening
\path{Core.PRFO}; from that point on, unqualified names such as
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{C}, \AgdaInductiveConstructor{P},
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{ind}, and \AgdaDatatype{→ᴾ} refer to the
paramorphism-primitive first-order syntax.  Later excerpts use the same
convention, with the fully qualified modules shown at the top of the
snippet.

{\small
\CorePRFOParamorphismFoldDerivedP*
}

each recursive result is paired with the subterm rebuilt from the
functor layer:

}

\section{Instances}
\label{sec:instances}

The core calculi are intentionally small; the instance layer shows that
they are expressive enough to host the concrete source languages that
motivated the development.  Each first-order instance follows the same
pattern.  A source language defines its own typed syntax and evaluator.
The instance module chooses a closed \PRFO{} type for the source data,
defines a structurally recursive compiler
\(\AgdaFunction{compile}\), and proves that the compiled arrow has the
same meaning as a direct categorical denotation in every \PRFO{} model.
For a source program \(p\), the characteristic theorem has the shape
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:instance-preservation}
  \AgdaFunction{interpret}\;(\AgdaFunction{compile}\;p)
  \;\approx_M\;
  \AgdaFunction{denote}\;p .
\end{equation}
Thus the compiler is not validated only by the executable Agda set
semantics; it is validated parametrically for all models of the target
calculus.

Several sources also carry an independently generated source equality.
For these languages, the corresponding equation module proves a theorem
named \AgdaFunction{preserves}: a source equation is mapped to the
generated equality of the target calculus.  These equation theorems are
separate from the semantic correctness theorem above; they say that the
compiler respects the source equational theory syntactically, before
choosing any particular model.

\paragraph{Natural numbers.}
The natural-number instance embeds the original \AgdaFile{PR-Nat}
language.  The target type is \(\mu X.\,1+X\), written
\AgdaFunction{Nat}; zero and successor compile to the two injections
followed by \AgdaInductiveConstructor{con}.  Composition compiles to
categorical composition and tupling.  The two primitive-recursion
constructors in the legacy source, one written as a paramorphism and one
as a fold-style recursor, are both compiled to the paramorphism primitive
of \PRFO{} by building the appropriate case handler over the one-step
unfolding of natural numbers.  The theorem \AgdaFunction{preserves} in
the nested \AgdaModule{Semantics} module states that interpretation of
the compiled \PRFO{} arrow agrees with the direct source denotation in an
arbitrary \PRFO{} model.

\paragraph{The category \(\mathbf{PREC}\).}
The Nat instance also connects the development to the standard
category of primitive recursive maps, matching the categorical view in
which primitive recursive functions are exactly the definable maps of
the initial arithmetic category~\cite{ye2026:_categ_struct_coher_theor_arith}.
\AgdaFile{Core.Instances.PREC} presents \(\mathbf{PREC}\) with objects
natural-number arities and morphisms \(n \to m\) given by \(m\)-tuples
of \AgdaFile{PR-Nat} programs of arity \(n\).  Identity is the tuple of
projections, and composition maps each output program over the input
tuple.  The companion module \AgdaFile{Core.Instances.Equations.PREC}
proves the category laws extensionally, using the source evaluator, and
defines the finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat} subcategory of the \PRFO{} target
on objects \(\AgdaFunction{vec}\ \AgdaFunction{Nat}\ n\).  The Nat
compiler is the arrow part of a functor from this presented
\(\mathbf{PREC}\) category to the finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat} target
subcategory: the theorems \AgdaFunction{F-id} and
\AgdaFunction{F-∘} show that \AgdaFunction{compile*}
preserves identities and composition in the generated \PRFO{} equality.
This bridge does not claim a converse completeness theorem saying that
every finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat} \PRFO{} arrow is represented by a
\AgdaFile{PR-Nat} program.

\paragraph{Words.}
The word instance handles primitive recursion over words.  For an
alphabet of size \(k\), the target is the initial algebra for a signature
with one nullary constructor for the empty word and one unary constructor
for each letter.  The compiler maps the empty-word constant and letter
constructors to the corresponding \PRFO{} constructors, projections to
product projections, and the word recursor to a paramorphism whose
handler has one branch for the empty word and one branch for each letter.
The same module also provides a wrapper for an arbitrary finite alphabet
\(A\): a finite presentation supplies encode/decode maps between \(A\)
and \(\AgdaDatatype{Fin}\;k\), and the compiler targets the corresponding
\(k\)-letter word type.

\paragraph{Ranked and many-sorted trees.}
The ranked-tree instance compiles a source language over a ranked
signature.  Such a signature determines the polynomial
\(\sum_{c\in\Sigma} X^{\mathsf{ar}(c)}\); the target type is the
corresponding \PRFO{} tree type, and each source constructor compiles to
the generic constructor arrow \AgdaFunction{conᴾ}.  The paramorphism case
uses a generic handler built from the rank vector, so each constructor
branch receives the recursive results and the original children in the
order expected by the source evaluator.

The many-sorted instance extends this pattern to many-sorted signatures.
The source language is intrinsically sorted: programs carry their input
and output sorts in their indices, and environments are sorted vectors of
terms.  The target \PRFO{} type erases the sort index to a single tagged
tree algebra \AgdaFunction{Target}; source typing is still used to
construct only well-sorted programs, while the compiler itself reuses the
ranked-tree compiler after erasing the sort indices.  The preservation
theorem again states that the compiled arrow agrees with the direct
sorted denotation in every \PRFO{} model.

\paragraph{Representation theorems.}
The model-level preservation schema in
Equation~\ref{eq:instance-preservation} says that the categorical
compiler commutes with categorical denotation.  The representation
modules give the corresponding executable correctness statements for the
concrete set interpreter.  That interpreter is backed by
\AgdaFile{Core.Semantics.Inductive}, which implements the closed
inductive carriers as a W-style fixed point of container descriptions,
and by a separate evaluator for the supported flat \PRFO{} fragment.  The
representation modules define encodings of source values as elements of
the compiled target type, prove that these encodings satisfy the relevant
logical relation \(\AgdaFunction{Related}\), and use the fundamental
theorem \(\AgdaFunction{correct}\) from the corresponding concrete module.
The theorem \(\AgdaFunction{correct}\) says that, for any related input
environment, evaluating the supported compiled \PRFO{} program produces a
result related to the source evaluator's result.  The representation
modules then specialize this theorem to encoded inputs as
\AgdaFunction{compiler-correct-on-representations} for natural numbers,
words, ranked trees, and many-sorted trees.

\paragraph{System T}
The higher-order instance translates the legacy System~T syntax into
contextual \PRHO{} and then into point-free \PRHO{}.  Source types
translate by sending \(\mathsf{Nat}\) to \(\mu X.\,1+X\) and function
types to \PRHO{} exponentials; contexts translate to products.  Variables
become projections, lambda abstraction and application become
curry/evaluation, numerals are built from zero and successor, and the
System~T recursor elaborates to the \PRHO{} paramorphism over natural
numbers.  The module proves that the contextual translation reifies back
to the elaborated contextual term, that contextual equations are
preserved by compilation, and that in any \PRHO{} model the interpreted
point-free arrow agrees with a direct structural denotation of the
System~T term.  The companion module
\AgdaFile{Core.Instances.Equations.SystemT} equips the same intrinsic
System~T syntax with generated source equations, including function
beta/eta, congruence and substitution closure, and the natural-number
recursor equations, and proves a direct preservation theorem
\(\AgdaFunction{preserves}\) into
\AgdaFile{Core.Equations.PRHO}.  The recursor cases use the PR-HO
structural computation laws for \AgdaInductiveConstructor{fmap} and
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{strength} on the natural-number functor, so the
module does not introduce a private target equality.  For the executable set model, the module defines a
logical relation over translated System~T types and proves its
fundamental theorem \(\AgdaFunction{concrete-correct}\): related
environments are mapped to related results.  The closed natural-number
corollary \AgdaFunction{closed-nat-correct} states that a closed System~T
numeral program computes to the concrete encoding of the legacy
evaluator's result.

\section{Formalization Design}
\label{sec:formalization-design}

Several design choices are visible in the Agda artifact but easy to miss
from the mathematical presentation alone.

\paragraph{Intrinsic point-free targets.}
The core calculi are intrinsically typed: the source and target objects
of an arrow are indices of the Agda datatype, so ill-typed target arrows
cannot be constructed.  This is convenient for categorical syntax,
because products, coproducts, exponentials, functorial action, strength,
and recursion all have their categorical arities in their constructors.
It also means that the compilers produce target arrows directly, rather
than producing untyped syntax plus a separate typing derivation.  The
shared type grammar is indexed by a first-order/higher-order mode, which
lets the first-order and higher-order developments share substitution and
renaming while preventing exponentials from appearing in first-order
types.
The inductive presentation also identifies an algebraic signature: the
same operations could be packaged as record interfaces for models and
homomorphisms, while retaining raw datatypes as the initial syntax.

\paragraph{Contextual syntax as a front end.}
The artifact also keeps contextual calculi for the cases where ordinary
typed syntax is the right interface.  Their judgement
\(\Gamma \vdash A\) can be read as the simply typed fragment of a
category with families in the sense of
Dybjer~\cite{Dybjer1996InternalTypeTheory}; more precisely, it is close
to the simply typed CwFs of
Castellan, Clairambault, and Dybjer~\cite{CastellanClairambaultDybjer2021CwF}.
Contexts are closed objects, substitutions are arrows
\(\Delta \to \Gamma\), terms are arrows \(\Gamma \to A\), the empty
context is the terminal object, and context extension is product.
Since types are closed and do not depend on terms, reindexing of types is
trivial; this is a constant-family CwF-style presentation, not a
dependent CwF.

This bridge is used concretely by the System~T instance.  Its
translation is written against contextual \PRHO{}, because that is the
natural shape of simply typed lambda terms, while the resulting programs
still live in the same point-free target category as the other
instances.  The module \AgdaFile{ContextualPRHO} in
\AgdaFile{Core.Translations} defines compile and reify maps between the
two presentations, proves that they are mutually inverse on raw syntax,
and proves that both maps preserve the independently generated
equations.  The contextual syntax is therefore not a second semantics,
but a CwF-style interface to the same target category described in
Section~\ref{sec:target-categories}.

\paragraph{Setoids instead of quotients.}
The equational theories are generated inductively over raw arrows, and
the model interfaces carry an explicit equality relation with the
required laws.  The corresponding homs are packaged as standard-library
setoids.  This avoids quotient types and keeps all code in Agda's safe
fragment, at the cost of making equality preservation and congruence
proofs explicit.  That tradeoff is deliberate: it keeps the syntax
inspectable and executable while still letting the paper state the
categorical results in terms of hom-setoids.

\paragraph{Type codes, guardedness, and containers.}
The syntax of types is intentionally larger than the class of type codes
that should be used to form inductive objects.  The artifact therefore
separates raw \AgdaDatatype{Ty} codes from validated codes: guardedness
checks that the recursive variable occurs below a data constructor, and
well-formedness checks this condition recursively under nested
\AgdaInductiveConstructor{ind}.  Clients validate type codes at language
boundaries instead of assuming that every raw code is productive.  The
set interpretation maps type codes to indexed containers, and closed
inductive types are interpreted by a W-style fixed point of those
containers.  This gives executable constructor, destructor, fold, and paramorphism
operations without using termination or positivity pragmas.  The
formalization also records which codes are polynomial: first-order codes
are polynomial, while higher-order exponentials may introduce infinitely
many positions.  This container-based route replaces the earlier
prototype's induction-recursive generic algebra; induction-recursion is
a standard way to organize such initial-algebra
constructions~\cite{DBLP:journals/apal/DybjerS03}, but it is not needed
in the checked core presented here.

\paragraph{Two primitive recursion presentations.}
The artifact contains both paramorphism-primitive and fold-primitive
calculi, rather than hiding the choice behind one overloaded interface.
This creates some parallel definitions and proofs, but it makes the
presentation-specific laws explicit and lets the translations of
Section~\ref{sec:paramorphisms-folds} be stated as checked
interdefinability theorems.  In particular, the first-order and
higher-order P/F results are not informal meta-level observations: they
are equation-preserving translations with round trips in the generated
equational theories.

\paragraph{Infrastructure.}
Distributivity is treated as categorical infrastructure, not as a source
feature.  In the first-order target it is a
primitive operation because bicartesian first-order structure alone does
not provide exponentials.  In \PRHO{}, the distributivity arrow is
derived from the exponential structure, and
Section~\ref{sec:prho-model-distributivity} proves that this derivation
is valid in every \PRHO{} model.  This design keeps the model interface
honest: higher-order models need not provide distributivity as an extra
field.

\paragraph{Checked entry point.}
The module \AgdaFile{Everything.agda} is the trusted entry point for the
new development.  It imports the core syntax, equations, model
interfaces, set semantics, contextual syntax, translations, and verified
instances, all under Agda's \AgdaKeyword{--safe} option.  Legacy bridge
modules that rely on older unsafe or termination-marked code are kept
outside this aggregate; they are useful for comparison, but they are not
part of the checked claim made by the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

The historical baseline is the classical theory of primitive recursive
and general recursive functions~\cite{skolem23,curry41:_formal_recur_arith,kleene36:_gener_recur_funct_natur_number}.
Our first-order instances keep the ordinary textbook presentation of
primitive recursion on natural numbers~\cite{martin2019logik,wiki:Primitive_recursive_function},
but factor it through a typed categorical target.  The use of folds and
related recursion schemes follows the categorical-programming line of
work on catamorphisms and higher-order
folds~\cite{DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerFP91,DBLP:conf/fpca/MeijerH95}.

On the semantic side, the first-order core uses distributive categories
in Cockett's sense~\cite{cockett_1993}, while the higher-order core uses
the cartesian-closed construction of distributivity studied by
Benini~\cite{https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.1406.0961}.  The
higher-order instance is related to G\"odel's System~T~\cite{goedel58}
and to presentations of System~T in modern type-theoretic
texts~\cite{DBLP:books/cu/Ha2016}.  The Agda formalization itself is in
the tradition of mechanized metatheory in dependently typed languages,
using Agda as introduced by Norell~\cite{DBLP:conf/afp/Norell08}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The mechanization establishes a small family of point-free categorical
calculi for primitive recursion, their generated equational theories,
law-bearing setoid models, interpretation soundness, translations between
paramorphism-primitive and fold-primitive presentations, and verified
source-language instances.  The instance layer now includes semantic
correctness for the concrete sources, equation-preservation theorems for
the generated source equalities, and a functorial bridge from
\(\mathbf{PREC}\) into the finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat} subcategory of the
\PRFO{} target.

Several natural extensions remain outside the checked claim.  The
\(\mathbf{PREC}\) bridge is one-way; a completeness theorem would show
that every finite-\AgdaFunction{Nat} \PRFO{} arrow is represented by a
\AgdaFile{PR-Nat} program.  The contextual calculi already look like a
simply typed CwF fragment, but a reusable CwF interface and a genuinely
dependent CwF account would require further refactoring.  Finally,
mixed-variance bifunctor structure may be a better long-term basis for
higher-order type operators than the current unary structural-functor
interface, especially if the development is extended beyond the present
closed-domain exponentials.
